Garden Tiger Moth (Binomial Name: Arctia caja)

This moth's caterpillar is sometimes called a "woolly bear" because of its furry body. Their fur can be slightly irritating for sensitive skin.

  • Host Plants - Nettle, foxglove, and a variety of other plants growing in gardens and meadows since they are generalists (consuming different types of plants)
  • Habitat - Gardens, meadows, and woodlands
  • Distribution - Northern parts of United States, Canada, Europe, Northern Asia, and Central Asia

Grey Dagger Moth (Binomial Name: Acronicta psi)

These are called "dagger" moths because of the black dagger-like markings on the forewings.

  • Host Plants - Various trees, such as oak, hawthorn, birch, and elm
  • Habitat - Most habitats, particularly woodland, hedgerows and gardens
  • Distribution - England, Wales, Ireland, Channel Islands, and sometimes Northern Europe

Buff Tip Moth (Binomial Name: Phalera bucephala)

These moths quite literally have a "buff tip", hence the name.

  • Host Plants - Mainly birch, hazel, oak, and sallow; also seen near alder, beech, elm, hornbeam, lime, rowan, and sycamore plants
  • Habitat - Gardens, hedgerows, scrub, and open woodlands
  • Distribution - Europe, Asia up to eastern Siberia

Cabbage White Butterfly (Binomial Name: Pieris rapae)

These butterflies are referred to as "cabbage white" because their caterpillars are pests to cabbage, kale, bok choy and broccoli. The "white" refers to the color of the butterfly's wings.

  • Host Plants - Plants in the family Cruciferae (cabbage, cauliflower, broccoli, etc.)
  • Habitat - Any type of open space, including meadows, bogs, forests, and fields
  • Distribution - Everywhere in the world with temperate climates

Peacock Butterfly (Binomial Name: Aglais io)

This butterfly is commonly called a "peacock" butterfly because of it's wings, which resemble a male peacock's feathers.

  • Host Plants - Common nettle, small nettle, and hop
  • Habitat - Gardens, parks, and woods
  • Distribution - British Isles, Europe, and temperate Asia as far east as Japan

BEWARE!

The following caterpillar species are VENOMOUS, so be careful and avoid them!

American Dagger Moth (Binomial Name: Acronicta Americana)

This moth is often mistaken for the regular Dagger Moth, but these are two different species! The hairs of the caterpillar are known to irritate human skin upon stinging, requiring medical intervention.

  • Host Plants - Maple, boxelder, alder, birch, hickory, chestnut, poplar, oak, basswood, elmus, and American hornbeam
  • Habitat - Forests and deciduous woodlands
  • Distribution - North America, particularly to the eastern part of the Rocky Mountains

Saddleback Moth (Binomial Name: Acharia stimulea)

This venomous caterpillar has a potent poison that destroys blood cells. Even a small prick from the poisonous spine can cause asthma, stomachache, and bleeding.

  • Host Plants - Cherry, elm, oak, plum, basswood, chestnut, maple, hackberry, spicebush, mountain coffee, grapevine, holly, sweet corn, and false buckthorn
  • Habitat - Forest areas where their host plants grow
  • Distribution - Eastern North America

Southern Flannel Moth (Binomial Name: Megalopyge opercularis)

The poison of the mature caterpillar is highly dangerous and can destroy blood cells. People who touch the animal may develop bleeding, difficulty breathing, and other dangerous symptoms.

  • Host Plants - Elm, oak, rose, ivy, and wild plum
  • Habitat - Gardens or forests where their host plants grow
  • Distribution - Eastern parts of the U.S. touching southeastern Virginia, and Florida, southern U.S., Mexico, and certain portions of Central America
